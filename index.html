Welcome

A Word from the Director歡迎

"Ever since watching the original, performed by the Anhui Huangmei Opera Troupe in Shanghai last year, The Female Prince Consort has tickled my brain with its catchy melodies and zany presence."

I am so pleased to welcome you to Red Threaded Theatre's first Oxford production, Prince Consort Ladygirl 女驸马. It seemed like the natural play to inaugurate Chinese Opera in Oxford.

Over the past four months I have had the pleasure and challenge of translating and adapting this script with a group of incredibly talented writers and poets — Coltrane Rogers, Lily Zhang, and Peregrine Neger. The physicality of Chinese Opera was aided greatly by our choreographer Jiasi Li, who led our actors in discovering their characters physically and educating them in the art of traditional Chinese Opera performance.

I must also extend a gracious thanks to my teacher at Shanghai Theatre Academy, Li Qiuping, and her legendary status as a Huadan actor and teacher. The actors I worked with on the first Red Threaded Theatre Production — Su Linyu, Li Peiluo, Hu Bei'er, Xu Lele, Chen Weitian, and Zhang Zhixin — gave me so many ideas and methods through our experiments in Chinese Opera.

For every actor in this performance, this is their first time performing traditional Chinese Opera, and I am so grateful for the amount of effort they have put into making this show so vibrant and authentic to the form.

We hope you have a brilliant time experiencing our Huangmei Xi performance, whether this is your first time watching traditional Chinese Opera or whether you're basically another Ouyang Yuqian. Enjoy the show!

William Want
Director, Red Threaded Theatre
The Story

Plot Summary劇情

"She sat the imperial examination in his name — and became the Kingdom's greatest scholar."

Feng Suzhen and Li Zhaoting meet and become engaged while studying together. But after Li's family falls into poverty, Feng Suzhen's parents force her to break off the match — and later falsely accuse him of theft, sending him to prison. Determined to save him, Feng Suzhen disguises herself as a man and travels to the capital, hoping to find her brother.

By chance, she sits the imperial examination in Li Zhaoting's name and unexpectedly becomes the Chief Wrangler — the top scholar in the Kingdom. While in the palace, she makes a startling discovery: her brother, Feng Shaoying, has disguised himself as a Concubine and is living undercover alongside the Royals.

The Emperor, impressed by her talent, orders her to marry the Princess. Trapped and bound by imperial decree, Feng Suzhen eventually confesses her true identity on their wedding night. Though initially furious, the Princess is moved — and persuades the Emperor to spare her.

Feng Suzhen is adopted as the Emperor's goddaughter. Li Zhaoting is restored and allowed to claim the top scholar's title. The Princess meets and falls in love with the disguised Shaoying — resulting in a happy ending and a flourishing double wedding.

Cultural Context

The History of Huangmei Xi黄梅戲

Origins · Folk Roots

The history of Huangmei Opera is long, complex, and not comprehensively documented. Emerging from eighteenth- and nineteenth-century folk song traditions in the border regions of Hubei, Jiangxi, and Anhui, it developed from small-scale, semi-improvised comic performance into a fully staged and orchestrated theatrical form by the turn of the twentieth century. After the founding of the People's Republic of China in 1949, further reforms institutionalised the genre and firmly established it as one of Anhui Province's representative operatic styles.

Early Form · The Two-Small Plays

In its earliest form, Huangmei Opera had no fixed role-types or standardised instrumentation. Performers sang folk songs and enacted dances at temple fairs and seasonal festivals. As simple narrative pieces emerged — including the so-called "two-small plays" (featuring a young male and young female role) and "three-small plays" (adding a clown role) — performances required rehearsal, costumes, and basic staging. This encouraged the formation of small, relatively stable troupes: often seasonal, loosely organised, and mobile, typically consisting of only seven or eight members.

Late Qing & Republican Era · Professionalisation

From the late Qing dynasty onward, Huangmei Opera became increasingly professionalised. Troupes expanded their repertory to include full-length dramas and absorbed musical and theatrical influences from neighbouring regional forms. By the early twentieth century, the genre had spread widely across western Anhui and surrounding areas, entering cities such as Anqing in 1932. After 1949, private troupes were reorganised into state-supported companies, improving performers' working conditions and enabling the preservation and adaptation of traditional repertoire.

1950s · The Golden Age & This Production

In the 1950s, works such as The Female Prince Consort achieved national prominence and were adapted for film. The following performance draws on the 1959 film adaptation, while also seeking to reintroduce performance elements associated with late Qing practice that have faded from many contemporary stagings.
